# Adab (Etiquette of the Path)

## Description
*Adab* (Arabic: أدب) means "etiquette," "proper conduct," or "refined comportment." In the Sufi context, it refers to the entire complex of right behavior — outward and inward — required of a seeker on the spiritual path. Adab is not merely politeness but a comprehensive discipline: the correct attitude toward God, toward the master (*shaykh*), toward fellow seekers (*ikhwan*), and toward oneself.

Sufi manuals (*adab al-murids*, "etiquette of the novices") specify adab in extraordinary detail: how to enter and leave the master's presence, how to eat and sleep in the *khanqah* (Sufi lodge), how to listen during dhikr or sama, how to treat the master's words, and how to conduct oneself in silence.

## Spiritual Purpose
The spiritual logic of adab is that improper conduct reveals — and reinforces — ego (*nafs*). The ego claims its own space, rights, and preferences; adab systematically dismantles these claims by requiring constant attentiveness to others and to God. In this sense, adab is a form of practical fana — the daily erosion of self-insistence.

Al-Junayd said: "We did not take Sufism from words, but from hunger, abandonment of the world, and cutting off comfortable habits." Adab is the structured container within which such cutting off occurs. A famous Sufi axiom: "The whole of Sufism is adab" (*al-tasawwuf kulluhu adab*).

The master-disciple relationship (*murshid-murid*) is governed almost entirely by adab. The disciple's unconditional submission (*taslim*) to the master is not mere obedience but a training of the will — learning to surrender the ego's preferences in small things before it can dissolve in the greater surrender of fana.

## Variations Across Orders
- **Qadiriyya and most orders:** Adab toward the shaykh includes specific greetings, postures, and forms of address; visiting the tomb of the founding shaykh is an act of adab to lineage.
- **Chishtiyya:** The Chishti tradition is notable for its *adab* toward guests (*mehmani*) as a spiritual practice — hospitality as service to the Divine manifested in the guest.
- **Naqshbandiyya:** Emphasizes inner adab (*adab al-batin*): the disciple must maintain constant inner attention (*muraqaba*) and never allow the heart to wander from awareness of God.
- **Mevleviyya:** Adab is embedded in the entire ceremonial structure of the *sema*: each gesture, garment, and movement carries symbolic meaning and must be performed correctly.

## Scholarly Debates
- **Adab vs. Shari'a:** Some reformist critics argue that Sufi adab creates an alternative moral hierarchy (loyalty to the shaykh over other obligations) that can conflict with or override the requirements of Islamic law. Sufi masters typically insist that true adab never violates Shari'a.
- **Blind obedience:** The *murshid-murid* adab has been criticized — by both Islamic reformers and secular scholars — for fostering unhealthy dependency or enabling the abuse of authority by fraudulent shaykhs.
- **Institutionalization:** As Sufism institutionalized into formal orders, adab codes became more elaborate and formalized. Some scholars (e.g., Trimingham) argue this formalization replaced inner spirituality with outer propriety.
