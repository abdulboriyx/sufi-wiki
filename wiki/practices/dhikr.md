# Dhikr (Remembrance of God)

## Description
*Dhikr* (Arabic: ذكر, also transliterated *zikr*) means "remembrance" or "recollection" — the practice of repeating divine names, Quranic phrases, or prayers as a form of spiritual discipline. The most common form is the repetition of *La ilaha illa Allah* (There is no god but God) or the single name *Allah*, but formulas vary widely by order and lineage.

Dhikr may be:
- **Silent (*dhikr khafi*):** Performed internally, in the heart, without moving the lips.
- **Vocal (*dhikr jahri*):** Spoken aloud, sometimes in rhythmic, melodic, or chanted form.
- **Collective (*dhikr jama'i*):** Performed in a circle (*halqa*) by members of a Sufi order, often with breathing techniques and physical movement.

## Spiritual Purpose
Dhikr is the central spiritual practice of virtually all Sufi orders. Its purpose, following Quran 13:28 ("Verily, in the remembrance of God do hearts find rest"), is to purify the heart (*qalb*), quiet the lower self (*nafs*), and orient consciousness toward God. At its highest levels, dhikr is not merely a practice but a state: the mystic is always "remembering" God, whether speaking or silent, awake or asleep.

In the station system (*maqamat*), dhikr is both a technique for advancing through stations and a characteristic of high stations themselves — the realized saint lives in a condition of perpetual remembrance.

## Variations Across Orders
- **Qadiriyya:** Uses loud (*jahri*) dhikr; the formula *La ilaha illa Allah* repeated in a distinctive rhythmic style.
- **Naqshbandiyya:** Distinctive for its insistence on *silent* (*khafi*) dhikr of the heart alone — considered by the order to be the more inward and powerful form.
- **Chishtiyya:** Incorporates dhikr into the *sama* (musical gathering); practitioners may enter altered states (*hal*) during collective dhikr.
- **Mevleviyya:** The *sema* ceremony combines movement (the whirling) with implicit dhikr; some consider the entire ceremony an embodied form of remembrance.

## Scholarly Debates
- **Permissibility of vocal/collective dhikr:** Some Sunni jurists and reformers (e.g., Wahhabi/Salafi tradition) consider collective vocal dhikr to be an impermissible innovation (*bid'a*), not practiced by the early community (*salaf*). Sufi scholars respond that the Quran commands remembrance without restricting its form.
- **Breathing techniques and postures:** Some orders accompany dhikr with specific breathing patterns and head movements (*lataif* system in Naqshbandiyya). Critics see this as unwarranted addition; practitioners argue it is a transmitted (*talqin*) technique.
- **Efficacy and mechanism:** Classical Sufi psychology (al-Ghazzali, al-Qushayri) explains dhikr's effect through the purification of the heart's mirror; modern scholars debate whether the altered states associated with intensive dhikr are psychological, neurological, or genuinely spiritual phenomena.
