# Sama (Mystical Audition / Spiritual Concert)

## Description
*Sama* (Arabic: سماع, "listening" or "hearing") is the practice of spiritual listening to music, poetry, and song as a means of awakening the heart and inducing spiritual states (*ahwal*). In its formal Sufi context, sama typically involves the recitation or singing of devotional poetry — often on themes of divine love, longing, and union — accompanied by instruments such as the ney (reed flute), rebab (bowed lute), or frame drum.

The participants are not passive listeners: the aim is to allow the music to move the inner self, and practitioners may weep, cry out, or enter states of ecstasy (*wajd*) during the gathering. Physical movement, including swaying or rising to dance, may follow. The famous whirling ceremony of the Mevleviyya is the most internationally known form.

## Spiritual Purpose
The rationale for sama rests on the belief that music and beautiful sound can bypass the rational mind and speak directly to the spiritual heart. The Arabic root *s-m-'* (to hear/listen) carries the sense of attentive, receptive listening — not entertainment but concentrated spiritual absorption.

Rumi's poetry frequently uses the reed flute (*ney*) as a symbol of the soul's separation from its divine origin: the music of the ney is itself a form of divine speech (*sama' ilahi*). The Mevlevi *sema* is understood as a cosmic enactment of the soul's journey toward reunion with God.

## Variations Across Orders
- **Mevleviyya:** The most formalized sama tradition. The *sema* ceremony is a carefully structured ritual with specific costumes, music, and movements; it has been designated by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage.
- **Chishtiyya (South Asia):** *Qawwali* music is the primary vehicle of sama; Chishti gatherings (*mehfil-e-sama*) are celebrated for their intensity. The Sabri Brothers and Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan represent this tradition in the 20th century.
- **Qadiriyya and others:** Many orders permit sama under regulated conditions; some branches prohibit it.
- **Naqshbandiyya:** Historically more cautious or opposed to sama; some branches prohibit music outright, relying instead on silent dhikr.

## Scholarly Debates
See also: [sama-controversy](../debates/sama-controversy)

- **Permissibility debate (classical):** Al-Ghazzali devoted a long section of the *Ihya'* to defending the permissibility of sama under proper conditions: the listener must be trained (*ahl al-sama'*), the gathering must be free from worldly entertainment intent, and no unlawful mixing of sexes should occur. Ibn al-Jawzi (d. 1200) wrote a rebuttal specifically condemning sama.
- **Effect vs. cause of spiritual states:** A core question: does sama *cause* spiritual states (*wajd*), or merely reveal states already present in the listener? Al-Ghazzali argues it is a revealer, not a cause — the music draws out what the heart already carries.
- **Risk of simulation:** Classical Sufi manuals warn against "simulated ecstasy" (*tawajud*) — performing outward signs of wajd without genuine inner experience. This is considered worse than no sama at all.
- **Modern reformist critique:** Salafi and Deobandi movements largely prohibit sama as an innovation or as a path to prohibited music; Sufi orders and their scholarly defenders maintain the classical permissibility arguments.
