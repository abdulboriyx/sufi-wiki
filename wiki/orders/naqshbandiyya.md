# Naqshbandiyya

## Founding and Lineage
The Naqshbandiyya is one of the most widespread and intellectually rigorous Sufi orders, tracing its origin to **Baha al-Din Naqshband** (1318–1389) of Bukhara (present-day Uzbekistan). The name *naqshband* means "engraver of the heart" — one who inscribes the divine name on the heart through silent remembrance.

The order's lineage is distinctive: it claims a "silent" (*khafi*) chain (*uwaysi silsila*) through Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (bypassing Ali ibn Abi Talib), giving it a special affinity with the Sunni orthodox tradition and a different character from most other orders. This Abubakri lineage reinforced the order's emphasis on Shari'a compliance.

Key figures in the development of the order:
- **Khwaja Ahrar** (d. 1490) — involved the order in political patronage in Central Asia
- **Ahmad Sirhindi** (d. 1624, Mujaddid Alf-i Thani) — reformer who proposed *wahdat al-shuhud* against *wahdat al-wujud* and sought to realign Sufism with strict Shari'a
- **Shah Waliullah of Delhi** (d. 1762) — synthesizer of Naqshbandi and Sufi thought with hadith scholarship

## Geography
The Naqshbandiyya has the largest geographic spread after the Qadiriyya:
- **Central Asia** (Uzbekistan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan) — its historical heartland
- **Ottoman Turkey** and the Balkans
- **South Asia** (India, Pakistan) — especially influential through Sirhindi and Shah Waliullah
- **China** (Xinjiang) — the Khojas were Naqshbandi masters
- **Caucasus** — the order led resistance against Russian expansion in the 19th century (Imam Shamil was a Naqshbandi shaykh)
- **Kurdistan and Iraq**

## Distinctive Teachings and Practices
- **Silent dhikr (*dhikr khafi*):** The most distinctive feature — the divine name is recited internally, in the heart, without movement of lips or vocalization. This is considered the most inward and powerful form of remembrance.
- **Muraqaba (spiritual watchfulness/meditation):** The Naqshbandiyya places particular emphasis on *muraqaba* — sustained, concentrated inner attention to the divine presence. This resembles contemplative meditation in structure.
- **Sobriety (*sahw*):** The order strongly favors the "sober" Sufi path: ecstatic expressions, intoxicated states, and unconventional behavior are viewed with suspicion. The mystic after fana returns to the world and fulfills all religious duties.
- **Shari'a compliance:** More than most orders, the Naqshbandiyya insists on rigorous adherence to Islamic law as inseparable from the mystical path.
- **Anti-sama tendency:** Many Naqshbandi branches prohibit sama and music, in contrast to the Chishtiyya and Mevleviyya.

## Historical Influence
The Naqshbandiyya has been politically consequential in a way unusual for Sufi orders. It led or inspired Muslim resistance movements against Russian expansion in the Caucasus and Central Asia, anti-British activity in India, and continues to be a major intellectual force in Islamic reform movements globally. Its insistence on the compatibility of inner mystical experience with strict outer law made it a bridge between Sufi and Salafi-adjacent reformist tendencies — though this bridge has always been contested.
