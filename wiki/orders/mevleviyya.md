# Mevleviyya (The Mevlevi Order)

## Founding and Lineage
The Mevleviyya (Turkish: Mevlevîlik; also known in the West as the "Whirling Dervishes") was founded in Konya (present-day Turkey) after the death of **Jalaluddin Rumi** (1207–1273) by his son **Sultan Walad** (d. 1312), who institutionalized his father's teachings and practices into a formal order. The name derives from *Mevlana* ("Our Master"), the Turkish honorific for Rumi.

The order's *silsila* traces through Rumi to **Shams-i Tabrizi** (d. c. 1248) and back to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (and thus to the Prophet). Rumi's encounter with the wandering dervish Shams was the catalytic event of his spiritual transformation.

## Geography
The Mevleviyya was centered in Konya, Anatolia (present-day Turkey), and spread throughout the Ottoman Empire — Constantinople, the Levant, Egypt, and the Balkans. The Ottoman sultans were often patrons of the order. It never became a mass order in the same sense as the Qadiriyya or Chishtiyya, but wielded enormous cultural influence.

Following Atatürk's dissolution of all Sufi orders in Turkey in 1925, the order lost its formal status. It was permitted to revive as a "cultural association" in 1954, and today the *sema* ceremony is performed publicly in Konya and is recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage (2008).

## Distinctive Teachings and Practices
- **The Sema Ceremony:** The defining ritual of the order — a structured ceremony in which dervishes (*semazens*) wear white robes and tall conical hats and rotate in a precise, meditative whirl (*deveran*). The whirling is understood as an enactment of the soul's journey from ego-death to union with God.
  - The black cloak (*hırka*) represents the ego's tomb; removing it reveals the white robe of the purified soul.
  - The tall hat (*sikke*) represents the tombstone of the ego.
  - The rotation mimics the movement of the planets and atoms — the cosmic dance of all creation around the Divine Center.
- **Music:** The Mevlevi musical tradition (*Mevlevi ayin*) is among the most sophisticated in Islamic civilization, featuring the ney (reed flute) as the primary instrument — Rumi's central symbol of the soul's longing.
- **40-day retreat (*çile*):** Novices underwent a strict 1001-day apprenticeship including a 40-day seclusion in a small cell — a period of intensive service and inner purification.
- **Poetry as scripture:** Rumi's *Mathnawi* (6 books, ~25,000 couplets) and *Divan-i Shams* serve as the primary devotional and doctrinal texts.

## Historical Influence
The Mevleviyya shaped Ottoman court culture, art, and music for centuries. It served as a meeting point between Persian, Turkish, and Arab mystical traditions. In the modern era, Rumi's poetry has become the bestselling poetry in the United States (in translation by Coleman Barks and others), though scholars debate how accurately these translations convey the original.
