# Qadiriyya

## Founding and Lineage
The Qadiriyya is the oldest and most widespread Sufi order (*tariqa*), founded by **'Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani** (1077–1166) of Baghdad. Al-Jilani was a Hanbali jurist and preacher who became famous for his ascetic discipline and his public sermons in Baghdad. He did not explicitly organize a formal order; the Qadiriyya was institutionalized by his sons and disciples after his death.

The spiritual chain (*silsila*) of the Qadiriyya traces back through Ali ibn Abi Talib to the Prophet Muhammad, giving the order special prestige in the Arab world.

## Geography
The Qadiriyya is the most globally distributed Sufi order, with strong presence in:
- **Arab world** (Iraq, Syria, Egypt, North Africa)
- **West Africa** (one of the dominant orders; spread by al-Hajj Umar Tall and others)
- **South Asia** (Pakistan, India)
- **Southeast Asia**
- **Sub-Saharan Africa**
- **The Balkans and Ottoman territories**

## Distinctive Teachings and Practices
- **Loud (*jahri*) dhikr:** The Qadiriyya is associated with vocal, collective dhikr, often in a circle (*halqa*).
- **Devotion to the Founder:** Intense veneration of 'Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani as *ghawth al-a'zam* (the Greatest Helper) — the supreme spiritual pole (*qutb*) of his age and, in popular belief, of all time.
- **Tolerance and breadth:** The Qadiriyya generally exhibits theological flexibility and has accommodated a wide range of local practices across cultures.

## Historical Influence
The Qadiriyya shaped West African Islam profoundly, producing the Tijaniyya as an offshoot and competing with it for followers. In South Asia it was the dominant order before the Chishtiyya rose to prominence. 'Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani's shrine in Baghdad remains one of the most visited in the Islamic world.
