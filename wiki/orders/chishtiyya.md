# Chishtiyya

## Founding and Lineage
The Chishtiyya is the dominant Sufi order of South Asia, founded by **Mu'in al-Din Chishti** (c. 1141–1236), who emigrated from Khorasan to the Indian subcontinent and settled in Ajmer (present-day Rajasthan, India). The order is named after the town of Chisht in present-day Afghanistan, where the lineage originated. Mu'in al-Din's successor **Qutb al-Din Bakhtiyar Kaki** (d. 1235) settled in Delhi, and **Farid al-Din Ganj-i Shakkar** (d. 1265) in the Punjab. The most famous Chishti master is **Nizam al-Din Awliya** (d. 1325) of Delhi, whose *malfuzat* (recorded conversations) are a major source of Chishti teaching.

## Geography
The Chishtiyya's heartland is the Indian subcontinent: India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh. It spread through Afghanistan and Central Asia via the founding figures but never achieved the global reach of the Qadiriyya.

## Distinctive Teachings and Practices
- **Sama and Qawwali:** The Chishtiyya is uniquely identified with *sama* as a regular spiritual practice. Chishti *khanqahs* held formal *mehfil-e-sama* (musical gatherings) as part of their weekly schedule. This gave rise to the *qawwali* tradition — Sufi devotional music that continues to this day.
- **Service to the poor:** The Chishti *khanqah* was famous as a center of unconditional hospitality; no one was turned away from the communal kitchen (*langar*), and feeding the poor was considered a supreme form of worship.
- **Withdrawal from political power:** Early Chishti masters systematically refused gifts from sultans and avoided the courts, preserving the order's independence and moral authority.
- **Love-centered theology:** The Chishtiyya emphasizes *mahabba* (love) as the path — the tradition is deeply influenced by Rumi, Hafiz, and devotional poetry.

## Historical Influence
The Chishtiyya played a decisive role in the Islamization of the Indian subcontinent — not through conquest but through service, music, and accessibility. Mu'in al-Din Chishti's shrine at Ajmer remains one of the most visited pilgrimage sites in South Asia, drawing both Muslim and non-Muslim devotees. Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan (d. 1997) brought Chishti qawwali to global audiences.
