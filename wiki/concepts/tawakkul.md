# Tawakkul (Absolute Trust in God)

## Definition
*Tawakkul* (Arabic: توكل) means absolute reliance on and trust in God (*tawakkala 'ala Allah*). In the Sufi *maqam* (station) system, it is a high station on the path, signifying the complete surrender of the will and the abandonment of self-planning in favor of divine providence.

## Origin and Development
Tawakkul is Quranic in origin: "And whoever relies upon Allah — then He is sufficient for him" (Quran 65:3). It was a defining virtue of early Sufi ascetics (*zuhhad*), particularly the school of Basra, who took it to extreme lengths — refusing to carry provisions, store food, or plan ahead at all.

Ibrahim ibn Adham (d. c. 782), the prince-turned-ascetic of Balkh, became the archetype of tawakkul: he abandoned a kingdom to trust entirely in God. Al-Muhasibi (d. 857) introduced a more nuanced psychological analysis, distinguishing tawakkul as an inner state from outer behavior.

Al-Ghazzali's treatment in the *Ihya'* is the most influential: he distinguishes three levels of tawakkul — trusting God while still using means; trusting God and accepting whatever comes with equanimity; and the highest, where the self is as a dead body in the washer's hands (*mayyit bayna yaday al-ghassil*), utterly passive to Divine action.

## Scholarly Disagreements
- **Extreme ascetics (early zuhhad tradition):** Tawakkul requires abandoning all worldly means (*asbab*) — not earning, not storing, not planning. To use means is to rely on other than God.
- **Moderate position (al-Ghazzali, mainstream Sufism):** Using worldly means (*asbab*) does not contradict tawakkul if the heart's dependence remains on God alone, not on the means. The *Sharia* requires earning one's livelihood; tawakkul is an inner disposition, not a rejection of normal life.
- **Juristic objection:** Some Sunni jurists argued that refusing to work or carry provisions when capable is prohibited (*haram*) and harms others — not a virtue.
- **Theological tension:** The extreme form of tawakkul risks fatalism (*jabr*) — a denial of human agency that Islamic theology generally rejects.
