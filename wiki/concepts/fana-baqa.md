# Fana and Baqa (Annihilation and Subsistence)

## Definition
*Fana* (Arabic: فناء) means annihilation or extinction — the dissolution of the individual ego (*nafs*) in the Divine. *Baqa* (Arabic: بقاء) means subsistence or permanence — the continued existence of the mystic after annihilation, now living "through God" rather than through the self. The two concepts form an inseparable pair: fana is not the end but the threshold to baqa.

## Origin and Development
The concepts were articulated systematically by Abu Yazid al-Bistami (d. 874), who described radical self-effacement in the Divine. Al-Junayd of Baghdad (d. 910) tempered this with the concept of "sobriety" (*sahw*): after annihilation, the mystic returns to the world with purified consciousness. This tension between intoxicated annihilation and sober subsistence became one of the central debates of Sufi thought.

Al-Ghazzali later integrated fana into a psychological and ethical framework in the *Ihya'*, grounding it in disciplined practice rather than ecstatic experience alone. Ibn Arabi gave it metaphysical elaboration: fana is not mere psychological dissolution but reflects the ontological truth that the self was never truly separate from God.

## Scholarly Disagreements
- **Classical Sufism (al-Junayd):** Fana is a psychological state, not literal merger. The mystic's individuality is preserved in baqa; only the lower ego is extinguished.
- **Ecstatic Sufism (al-Bistami, al-Hallaj):** The language of fana verges on identification with the Divine — "I am the Truth" (*Ana al-Haqq*) — which orthodox scholars condemned as heresy.
- **Theosophical Sufism (Ibn Arabi):** Fana is grounded in *wahdat al-wujud*: since only God truly exists, the self was always an illusion. Fana is the realization of this ontological truth.
- **Modern scholarship:** Some scholars (e.g., Massignon) see in fana a parallel to Christian mystical death; others (e.g., Sells) caution against such comparisons and emphasize the distinctly Islamic framework.
