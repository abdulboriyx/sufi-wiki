# Imagination (*Khayal*)

## Definition
In Akbari Sufism (the school of Ibn 'Arabi), *Khayal* (Arabic: خيال) or "Imagination" is not the production of "imaginary" or "unreal" things. Rather, it is an ontological reality — a bridge or "isthmus" (*barzakh*) between the world of pure meanings (*ma'ani*) and the world of sensory bodies (*ajsam*).

## The World of Imagination (*Alam al-Khayal*)
Ibn 'Arabi describes the *Alam al-Khayal* (also known as the *Alam al-Mithal* or World of Images) as a realm where spiritual realities take on forms and sensory things are spiritualized. It is the realm of:
- **Dreams:** Where the dreamer perceives meanings in the form of sensory images.
- **Visionary experience:** Where the mystic "sees" spirits or Divine Names in physical form.
- **The Afterlife:** The *Barzakh* between death and resurrection is described as having the same ontological properties as the World of Imagination.

## Macrocosmic and Microcosmic Imagination
- **Macrocosmic:** The entire universe is a work of Divine Imagination. God "imagined" the world, and thus it exists as a "shadow" of His Being. It is real because it is God's self-disclosure, but it is "imaginal" because it is not God's Essence.
- **Microcosmic:** The human faculty of imagination is a "ladder" that allows the seeker to perceive spiritual truths. It is the faculty that "binds" meanings to forms.

## Scholarly Interpretation
William Chittick (1989) argues that for Ibn 'Arabi, the "Metaphysics of Imagination" is the key to holding together God's transcendence (*tanzih*) and immanence (*tashbih*). Without imagination, God would be either purely abstract and unreachable or purely material and limited.
