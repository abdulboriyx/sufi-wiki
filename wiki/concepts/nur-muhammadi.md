# Nur Muhammadi (The Muhammadan Light) and Insan Kamil (The Perfect Man)

## Definition
*Nur Muhammadi* (Arabic: النور المحمدي) is the doctrine of the "Muhammadan Light" — the pre-eternal, cosmic spiritual reality of the Prophet Muhammad, understood as the first thing God created and the archetype of all creation. Related to this is the concept of *Insan Kamil* (Arabic: الإنسان الكامل), the "Perfect Man" — the human being who has fully realized the Divine names and attributes and acts as the mediating axis between God and creation.

## Origin and Development
The theological basis is the hadith: "The first thing God created was my light" (*awwal ma khalaqa Allahu nuri*) — though the authenticity of this hadith is debated by hadith scholars. The concept was developed by Sahl al-Tustari (d. 896) and later by al-Hallaj.

Ibn 'Arabi gave it full systematic form in *Fusus al-Hikam*, where each chapter treats a prophet as the "bezel" (*fass*) of a specific Divine name, and Muhammad as the seal encompassing all. The *Insan Kamil* is not merely the historical Muhammad but the realized mystic (the *qutb*, or pole) who embodies all Divine names.

'Abd al-Karim al-Jili (d. c. 1424) wrote the most detailed treatment in *Al-Insan al-Kamil* (Universal Man), extending the doctrine into a full cosmological system.

## Scholarly Disagreements
- **Mainstream Sufi tradition:** The Muhammadan Light is a legitimate expression of the Prophet's unique cosmic status and spiritual centrality to all creation.
- **Orthodox critics:** Elevating the Prophet to a pre-eternal, cosmic role risks shirk (associating partners with God) or conflicts with the Quranic statement that Muhammad is "only a messenger" (*rasul*). Ibn Taymiyya strongly rejected these elaborations.
- **Hadith scholars:** The foundational hadith on which the doctrine rests (*awwal ma khalaqa Allahu nuri*) is considered weak or fabricated by most hadith critics, though it remains widely cited in devotional literature.
- **Insider vs. outside scholarship:** From within the tradition, *Nur Muhammadi* is experienced as devotional truth; outside scholars (e.g., Schimmel, Sells) treat it as a mythological-theological construct expressing the Prophet's exemplary spiritual status.
