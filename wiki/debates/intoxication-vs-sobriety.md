# Intoxication vs. Sobriety (Sukr vs. Sahw)

## The Debate
One of the most fundamental divisions within Sufi thought concerns the proper disposition of the mystic who has approached or attained the state of nearness to God. The debate is framed as *sukr* (Arabic: سكر, "intoxication" or "drunkenness") versus *sahw* (Arabic: صحو, "sobriety" or "wakefulness").

**The question:** When the mystic is overwhelmed by the experience of divine proximity or love, should this be expressed openly — in ecstatic utterance, unconventional behavior, and transgressive language — or should the mystic maintain outward composure, discretion, and fidelity to the religious law?

This is not simply a practical question about behavior. It reflects deeper disagreements about:
- The nature of the highest mystical states
- The relationship between mystical experience and the Shari'a
- Whether esoteric truth can or should be expressed in public language
- The spiritual value of self-disclosure vs. self-concealment

---

## Position 1: The Defense of Intoxication (Sukr)

**Key figures:** Abu Yazid al-Bistami (d. 874), Mansur al-Hallaj (d. 922), Ruzbihan Baqli (d. 1209)

**The argument:**
- At the highest station of fana, the mystic's ego is annihilated. There is no longer a self capable of "controlling" expression. The *shath* (ecstatic utterance, *theopathic locution*) is not the mystic speaking but God speaking through the emptied vessel.
- Al-Hallaj's "Ana al-Haqq" (I am the Truth) is the paradigmatic example: he was not claiming divinity for himself (*shirk*) but reporting the annihilation of self in God. The utterance is simultaneously blasphemy to the uncomprehending and ultimate monotheism to the initiate.
- Al-Bistami reported ecstatic utterances (*shatahat*) such as "Glory be to Me!" (*Subhani*) — words normally reserved for God.
- Ruzbihan Baqli's *Sharh-i Shathiyyat* (Commentary on Ecstatic Utterances) is the classical defense of this language, arguing it should be interpreted esoterically, not literally.

**Supporting logic:**
- The intoxicated state is involuntary — it is given by God, not sought by the mystic. Suppressing it would be to suppress the divine gift.
- The laws of propriety are designed for the ego-self; when the self is gone, the laws no longer apply to the mystic in the same way.

---

## Position 2: The Defense of Sobriety (Sahw)

**Key figures:** Al-Junayd of Baghdad (d. 910), Al-Ghazzali (d. 1111), Ahmad Sirhindi (d. 1624)

**The argument:**
- Al-Junayd is the founder of the "sober" school. He argued that the highest station is not the annihilation of fana but the return in baqa — the mystic returns from intoxication with a purified and stabilized consciousness. The mature mystic does not lose control of expression.
- Al-Ghazzali in the *Ihya'* and *Mishkat al-Anwar* is cautious: ecstatic utterances are real experiences but should not be propagated publicly. Hallaj made the mistake of speaking what should be kept hidden.
- Sirhindi argued that the experience of *wahdat al-wujud* is a feature of the intoxicated *fana* state — it feels like union — but it is not the final truth. The sober state of *baqa* reveals that God and creation remain distinct. This epistemological argument uses the sobriety/intoxication distinction to critique Ibn Arabi's metaphysics.

**Supporting logic:**
- The Shari'a is binding on all Muslims; no mystical state removes this obligation. A state that leads to antinomianism is, at best, incomplete and, at worst, a temptation.
- Public ecstatic expression causes scandal, confusion, and harm to ordinary believers who lack the framework to interpret it correctly. Discretion (*kitman al-sirr*, guarding the secret) is itself a high virtue.
- The mature mystic models proper behavior precisely because the ego has been disciplined, not because the ego has been destroyed.

---

## Orthodox Position
From the perspective of Sunni orthodoxy (and especially Hanbali and later Salafi thought), both "intoxication" and "sobriety" as Sufi categories are suspect — but the intoxicated school is far more dangerous. Hallaj's execution was a judicial decision based on the principle that his utterances constituted *zandaqa* (heresy) regardless of the esoteric intent attributed to them. Ibn Taymiyya condemned both the ecstatic Sufis and, to a lesser degree, the sober ones if they maintained claims to special spiritual hierarchy.

---

## What the Debate Does NOT Resolve
- Whether the experience of *fana* is veridical — i.e., does it genuinely reveal the nature of reality, or is it a psychological altered state?
- Where the line is between genuine ecstatic annihilation and self-indulgent theatrics. Sufi manuals (e.g., al-Qushayri's *Risala*) worry about this constantly.
- Whether al-Hallaj should be read as a martyr of love or a cautionary example of indiscretion.
