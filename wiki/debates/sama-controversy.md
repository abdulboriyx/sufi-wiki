# The Sama Controversy

## The Debate
*Sama* — spiritual listening to music and poetry as a devotional practice — is one of the most contested practices in Islamic history. The debate has been active from the 9th century to the present and involves legal scholars (*fuqaha*), hadith specialists (*muhaddithun*), Sufi masters, and reformers. It is not a single debate but a cluster of overlapping questions:

1. **Is music itself permissible in Islam?** (The general music debate)
2. **Is sama specifically — as a Sufi spiritual practice — permissible?**
3. **Under what conditions, if any, is sama permissible?**
4. **Does sama cause or merely accompany spiritual states (*wajd*)?**
5. **What distinguishes genuine spiritual listening from entertainment?**

---

## Position 1: Sama is Permissible Under Proper Conditions

**Key figures:** Al-Ghazzali (d. 1111), al-Qushayri (d. 1074), most Chishti and Mevlevi masters

**The argument:**
- Al-Ghazzali's treatment in the *Ihya' 'ulum al-din* (Book 40, "On Sama and Ecstasy") is the most comprehensive classical defense. He distinguishes between:
  - **Haram sama:** Music performed for entertainment, lust, or worldly distraction — prohibited.
  - **Permitted or commendable sama:** Music that stirs the heart toward God, longing, and love — permissible or even meritorious for those capable of it.
- Eligibility matters: sama is only appropriate for *ahl al-sama'* (those fit for listening) — those with genuine spiritual development, sincere intention, and the inner state to respond properly. For the spiritually immature, sama is a door to distraction or worse.
- The gathering must be free from prohibited elements: no mixing of unrelated men and women, no wine, no instruments associated primarily with worldly amusement (classical jurists debated which instruments fell into this category).
- Al-Ghazzali's central philosophical argument: music does not *create* spiritual states but *reveals* what is already in the heart. If the heart carries love of God, music draws it out. If the heart carries base desires, music inflames them.

**Quranic and hadith support cited:**
- Quran 17:44: "There is nothing that does not glorify His praise" — all beautiful sound can be divine glorification.
- Reports of the Prophet hearing poetry and showing emotion.
- Reports of early Muslims (Companions and Successors) permitting singing.

---

## Position 2: Sama is Prohibited (Haram or Bid'a)

**Key figures:** Ibn al-Jawzi (d. 1200), Ibn Taymiyya (d. 1328), Ibn al-Qayyim (d. 1350), and the mainstream Hanbali, Deobandi, and Salafi/Wahhabi scholarly traditions

**The argument:**
- Most instruments are prohibited in Islam based on explicit hadith (e.g., "My community will have people who make lawful fornication, silk, wine, and musical instruments" — Bukhari). This prohibition is not lifted for Sufi purposes.
- Sama as practiced by Sufi orders involves specifically prohibited instruments (flute, stringed instruments) and creates an artificial environment of emotional stimulation that is spiritually dangerous, not beneficial.
- Ibn al-Jawzi wrote *Talbis Iblis* (The Devil's Deception) specifically targeting sama: he argues that Satan uses music precisely because it lowers the rational guard and makes sinful states feel spiritual. The "ecstasy" of sama is deception.
- Even if al-Ghazzali's conditions are met in theory, in practice they never are: sama gatherings inevitably involve mixing of sexes, alcohol, and the spiritual unprepared — the conditions that make it permissible are never actually achieved.
- The early community (*salaf*) did not practice sama as a formal spiritual exercise; therefore it is an innovation (*bid'a*).

---

## Position 3: Context-Dependent — Internal Sufi Disagreement

Not all Sufi orders agreed on sama:
- **Chishtiyya:** Strongly for sama; considered it central to spiritual development. Nizam al-Din Awliya defended it vigorously against critics.
- **Naqshbandiyya:** Generally against sama and music, relying on silent dhikr instead. Some Naqshbandi branches explicitly prohibit it.
- **Qadiriyya:** Mixed — some branches permit regulated sama, others do not.
- **Internal debates within the Mevleviyya:** Even defenders of the Mevlevi *sema* distinguished it from more emotionally uncontrolled forms of sama.

This internal diversity shows that the debate is not simply "Sufis vs. jurists" but runs through the Sufi tradition itself.

---

## The Question of Wajd (Ecstasy)
A specific sub-controversy concerns *wajd* — the ecstatic state that sama may induce:
- **Is wajd genuine?** Al-Qushayri and al-Ghazzali: yes, but only for the spiritually prepared. Wajd is an involuntary gift, not a performance.
- **Tawajud (simulated ecstasy):** Classical manuals agree this is blameworthy or prohibited — performing outward signs of wajd without inner reality.
- **Physical movement during wajd:** Is it permissible to rise and move or dance during sama? Al-Ghazzali: it is involuntary and should not be suppressed or performed deliberately. Ibn al-Jawzi: it is always performance.

---

## What the Debate Does NOT Resolve
- Whether the foundational hadith prohibiting instruments are sound — hadith scholars continue to debate their authenticity and interpretation.
- Whether the emotional states produced by sama are reliably spiritual vs. psychological — a question neither medieval nor modern scholarship has resolved.
- The line between "religious music" and "entertainment" — different cultures draw it differently, and the distinction shifts over time.
