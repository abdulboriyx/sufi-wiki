# Rabia al-Adawiyya
**Dates:** c. 717–801
**Origin:** Basra (Iraq)
**Order/Lineage:** No formal order; independent ascetic of the Basra school. Associated with the early *zuhhad* (ascetics) tradition but transcended it.
**Key Contribution:** Divine Love (*Mahabba* / *'Ishq*)

## Overview
Rabia al-Adawiyya is the most famous female saint in Islam and one of the most important figures in the entire history of Sufism. She transformed the early Sufi focus on asceticism (*zuhd*) and fear of God (*khawf*) into a focus on pure, selfless love (*mahabba*). Her contribution was not doctrinal but experiential: she reoriented the spiritual life around the positive pole of love rather than the negative pole of fear and guilt.

According to tradition, she was a freed slave who spent her life in prayer, fasting, and renunciation in Basra. She attracted disciples and is reported to have debated other spiritual figures of her time, including al-Hasan al-Basri.

## Core Teachings
- **Pure Love (*Mahabba*):** God should be loved for His own sake alone, not for hope of Paradise or fear of Hell. She distinguished between a "selfish" love that seeks reward, and a "worthy" love that forgets the self entirely.
- **The Prayer of Pure Love:** "O God, if I worship Thee for fear of Hell, burn me in Hell; and if I worship Thee in hope of Paradise, exclude me from Paradise; but if I worship Thee for Thine own sake, grudge me not Thine everlasting Beauty."
- **Asceticism as Background, Not Goal:** Unlike the early ascetics who valued *zuhd* as an end, Rabia treated asceticism as the context within which love flourishes, not the destination itself.

## Primary Works
Rabia left no known writings. Her teachings survive through:
- **Farid al-Din 'Attar**, *Tadhkirat al-Awliya'* (Memorial of the Saints, 12th century) — the main hagiographic source, written centuries after her death.
- **Al-Sulami**, *Dhikr al-Niswa al-Muta'abbidat al-Sufiyyat* (Remembrance of Female Sufi Devotees, 10th century).
- Scattered reports in al-Qushayri's *Risala* and other classical Sufi manuals.

## Controversies and Criticisms
- **Historicity:** The detailed accounts were written centuries after her death. Scholars (Margaret Smith, *Rabia the Mystic*, 1928; Rkia Cornell) debate how much is reliable biography vs. hagiographic elaboration.
- **Gender:** Her example was celebrated but also contained — acknowledged as a supreme saint while sometimes being used to limit her as a model for others (she did not lead congregational prayer, etc.).
- **Western parallels:** Some scholars noted parallels with Christian *amor purus* (pure love); the mainstream scholarly view is that the parallel is typological, not genealogical.
- **Status among male masters:** Classical sources show her debating al-Hasan al-Basri as an equal or superior; some later traditions subordinate her within male-dominated hierarchies.
