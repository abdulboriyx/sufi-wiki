# Mansur al-Hallaj
**Dates:** 858–922
**Origin:** Al-Baydha (Persia), later Baghdad
**Order/Lineage:** Disciple of al-Junayd of Baghdad (though later estranged from him), also influenced by Sahl al-Tustari. No continuous order traces itself directly to him.
**Key Contribution:** Martyrdom for Divine Love, *Ana al-Haqq*

## Overview
Al-Hallaj is the most controversial figure in Sufism. A "drunken" Sufi known for his ecstatic utterances (*shathiyat*), he was arrested, imprisoned for years, and finally publicly executed in Baghdad in 922 for saying "Ana al-Haqq" (I am the Truth). He became the paradigmatic martyr of divine love — the figure whose inner experience was so complete that it overwhelmed social and legal convention.

His name means "carder of cotton" — a trade he practiced — and Sufi poets later gave this symbolic meaning: as cotton is carded apart by the carder, so the mystic's ego is destroyed by God's love.

## Core Teachings
- **Ana al-Haqq (I am the Truth):** The most famous ecstatic utterance in Sufism. Often misread as a claim of personal divinity, it was understood by Hallaj and his defenders as the speech of God through an ego that has been annihilated (*fana*). The "I" that speaks is no longer the human ego but the Divine reality.
- **Satan as Monotheist:** In his *Kitab al-Tawasin*, he presented Iblis (Satan) as the perfect lover and pure monotheist — Iblis refused to prostrate before Adam because he would bow only to God. This provocative argument was meant to illustrate the absolute nature of divine love and loyalty, not to rehabilitate Satan.
- **Public proclamation of esoteric truth:** His willingness to speak the unspeakable publicly was itself a teaching — or a provocation. His teacher al-Junayd reportedly condemned this as irresponsible disclosure of secrets that should be kept hidden.

## Primary Works
- ***Kitab al-Tawasin*** — a short, dense mystical work of extraordinary difficulty, containing the Satan discussion and other theosophical meditations. Translated by Massignon.
- Poems (*diwan*) — attributed to him; many survive in later anthologies.

## Controversies and Criticisms
- **His execution:** Was it legally justified? Classical Muslim opinion is divided. Many orthodox scholars (including some Sufis) accepted the verdict — his public utterance constituted punishable heresy (*zandaqa*). Many Sufis saw it as martyrdom: he died for the truth of divine love.
- **Al-Junayd's condemnation:** His own teacher reportedly declared his blood lawful (*halal*) — a painful historical detail that illustrates the sobriety/intoxication divide (see [intoxication-vs-sobriety](../debates/intoxication-vs-sobriety)).
- **Louis Massignon's scholarship:** The French scholar devoted his career to Hallaj (*La Passion de Hallaj*, 1922), interpreting him in quasi-Christian terms as a mystical martyr who offered himself in expiation for humanity. This reading is contested as projecting Christian categories onto an Islamic figure.
- **Political dimension:** Some historians argue his execution was as much political as religious — he had followers among the poor and made powerful enemies at the Abbasid court.
