# Al-Ghazzali
**Dates:** 1058–1111
**Origin:** Tus (Khorasan, present-day Iran)
**Order/Lineage:** Shafi'i jurist and Ash'ari theologian; trained in Sufism under various masters including al-Farmadhi. Later associated with the sober Sufi tradition of al-Junayd.
**Key Contribution:** Synthesis of Orthodoxy and Sufism

## Overview
Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazzali was the most influential Islamic theologian and jurist after the classical period. At the height of his career as professor at the Nizamiyya madrasa in Baghdad, he experienced a spiritual crisis and abandoned his prestigious position to pursue the Sufi path — wandering for years in Syria, Palestine, and Mecca. His later writings synthesized Sufi experiential wisdom with orthodox Islamic theology and law, legitimizing Sufism for mainstream Sunni Islam.

Known as *Hujjat al-Islam* (Proof of Islam), he is one of the few figures accepted as authoritative by both Sufi and anti-Sufi currents within Sunni Islam — though each appropriates him differently.

## Core Teachings
- **The Heart (*Qalb*):** The heart as the organ of spiritual perception and knowledge — not the physical heart but the spiritual center of the human being. Purifying the heart through discipline is the purpose of all spiritual practice.
- **Stages and States (*Maqamat* and *Ahwal*):** Al-Ghazzali systematized the Sufi path into a coherent ethical and psychological framework, translating mystical experience into categories accessible to ordinary religious life.
- **The Niche for Lights (*Mishkat al-Anwar*):** A major philosophical work interpreting the Quranic "Light Verse" (24:35) and exploring the metaphysics of light, prophetic knowledge, and levels of spiritual vision.
- **Moderate Tawakkul:** He defended absolute trust in God while insisting it is compatible with using worldly means — a decisive intervention against extreme ascetic misreadings.

## Primary Works
- ***Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din*** (Revival of the Religious Sciences) — his masterwork, a vast compendium integrating law, theology, Sufi ethics, and spiritual psychology across 40 books. Considered by many the greatest work in Islamic literature after the Quran.
- ***Tahafut al-Falasifa*** (Incoherence of the Philosophers) — a critique of Aristotelian philosophy (*falsafa*) that became a landmark in Islamic intellectual history.
- ***Al-Munqidh min al-Dalal*** (Deliverance from Error) — his spiritual autobiography; the most important account of his crisis and conversion to Sufism.
- ***Mishkat al-Anwar*** (The Niche for Lights) — mystical commentary on divine light.
- ***Minhaj al-'Abidin*** (Path of the Worshippers) — shorter practical guide to the spiritual life.

## Controversies and Criticisms
- **Ibn Rushd's (Averroes) response:** Ibn Rushd wrote *Tahafut al-Tahafut* (Incoherence of the Incoherence), a systematic defense of philosophical method against al-Ghazzali's critique, arguing that al-Ghazzali misunderstood the philosophers he attacked.
- **Did he achieve genuine mystical realization?** Some later Sufis (e.g., Ibn 'Arabi) implicitly questioned whether al-Ghazzali's synthesis represented the highest spiritual attainment — he systematized the path but may not have reached its summit.
- **His condemnation of philosophy:** His attack on the philosophers (*falasifa*) for three allegedly heretical positions (eternity of the world, God's ignorance of particulars, denial of bodily resurrection) had long-term effects on Islamic intellectual life. Some historians argue it contributed to the decline of rationalist philosophy in the Islamic world — a contested and complex claim.
- **Hallaj and the intoxicated mystics:** Al-Ghazzali implicitly criticized the public expression of ecstatic states, suggesting Hallaj's error was disclosure (*ifsha' al-sirr*) — speaking the unspeakable. This is the "sober" Sufi position.
