# Jalaluddin Rumi
**Dates:** 1207–1273
**Origin:** Balkh (Khorasan, present-day Afghanistan); later settled in Konya (present-day Turkey)
**Order/Lineage:** Disciple of Shams-i Tabrizi (d. c. 1248); his son Sultan Walad founded the **Mevleviyya** order based on his teachings.
**Key Contribution:** *Mathnawi*, Mevlevi Order, poetry of divine love

## Overview
Jalaluddin Rumi — known in Persian as *Mawlana* ("Our Master") and in Turkish as *Mevlana* — is the most famous Sufi poet and one of the most widely read poets in the world. Born in Khorasan, he and his family fled westward ahead of the Mongol invasions, eventually settling in Konya (Rum — hence *Rumi*), then the capital of the Seljuk Sultanate of Anatolia.

Until his forties, Rumi was a respected Islamic scholar and jurist. His encounter with the wandering dervish **Shams-i Tabrizi** in 1244 was the catalyst for a total transformation: he abandoned his scholarly career, entered a state of perpetual spiritual intoxication, and began composing the poetry for which he is known.

## Core Teachings
- **The Reed Flute (*Ney*):** The opening of the *Mathnawi* — "Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale / complaining of separations" — presents the soul as a reed cut from the reed-bed of divine origin, whose every sound is a cry of longing for return. This is Rumi's central image for the spiritual condition of the human being.
- **Love (*'Ishq*) as the Path:** For Rumi, love is not merely a feeling but the fundamental force of the universe — divine love is what moves everything. The spiritual path is surrender to love rather than doctrinal mastery.
- **Shams-i Tabrizi:** Rumi's collection of ghazals (*Divan-i Shams-i Tabrizi*) is dedicated to Shams, often in language so intimate and intense that later interpreters debated whether it should be read as mystical or human love — a deliberate ambiguity for Rumi.
- **The Return (*Awda*):** The movement of the soul is separation, longing, and return — corresponding to the structure of fana and baqa.

## Primary Works
- ***Mathnawi-i Ma'nawi*** (Spiritual Couplets) — 6 books, approximately 25,000 couplets; an encyclopedic narrative poem weaving together stories, teachings, and spiritual commentary. Jami called it "the Quran in Persian."
- ***Divan-i Shams-i Tabrizi*** (also called *Kulliyat-i Shams*) — a large collection of lyric poems (*ghazals* and *rubais*) attributed to the encounter with Shams.
- ***Fihi ma Fihi*** (In It What Is In It) — a prose collection of his discourses.
- ***Maktubat*** — letters.
- ***Majalis-i Sab'a*** — seven sermons.

## Controversies and Criticisms
- **Translation distortions:** The explosion of Rumi's popularity in the West, especially through Coleman Barks's loose "versions" (not translations from the Persian), has been widely criticized by scholars (e.g., Jawid Mojaddedi, Franklin Lewis) as stripping his poetry of its Islamic context, theology, and language — producing a "universal mystic" divorced from his tradition.
- **The Shams relationship:** The intense, exclusive nature of his relationship with Shams caused scandal among his disciples and family. Shams disappeared (or was killed — possibly by Rumi's own disciples) twice. Some scholars read the relationship in terms of the master-disciple bond; others (including some in the tradition) interpret the poetry as expressing a human friendship of extraordinary spiritual depth.
- **Ottoman use and suppression:** The Mevlevi order was suppressed by Atatürk's government in 1925 as part of the secularization program; Rumi's texts were declared a "cultural heritage" of Turkey rather than a living religious tradition — a reframing his followers contest.
- **Universalism:** Within the Muslim world, Rumi's embrace of multiple faiths and his poetry's apparent theological pluralism has been celebrated by some and criticized by others as incompatible with Islamic exclusivism.
