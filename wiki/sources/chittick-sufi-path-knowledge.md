# The Sufi Path of Knowledge: Ibn al-Arabi's Metaphysics of Imagination
**Author:** William C. Chittick
**Published:** 1989, State University of New York Press

## Summary
*The Sufi Path of Knowledge* is a landmark systematic exposition of the thought of Muhyi al-Din ibn al-Arabi (1165–1240), known as "The Greatest Master" (*al-Shaykh al-Akbar*). Rather than a secondary analysis, Chittick provides a massive anthology of over 600 translated passages from Ibn al-Arabi's magnum opus, *al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya* (The Meccan Openings), organized into a coherent metaphysical and spiritual framework.

The book is structured into six parts: Overview, Theology, Ontology, Epistemology, Hermeneutics, and Soteriology. It remains the definitive English-language resource for understanding the technical vocabulary and philosophical depth of the Akbari tradition.

## Core Argument
Chittick argues that Ibn al-Arabi's thought is not a "philosophy" in the Western sense but a "verified" (*muhaqqaq*) knowledge derived from direct spiritual "opening" (*futuh*). The central thesis is that the universe is the constant self-disclosure (*tajalli*) of the Divine Being through the medium of the "Imaginal World" (*alam al-khayal*), and that the goal of human existence is to realize the state of the "Perfect Human" (*al-insan al-kamil*), who mirrors all the Divine Names.

## Key Ideas
### 1. Theology: Incomparability and Similarity
- **What is argued:** God must be understood through the tension between *tanzih* (transcendence/incomparability) and *tashbih* (immanence/similarity). 
- **Support:** Rooted in the Koranic verses "Nothing is like Him" (tanzih) and "He is the Hearing, the Seeing" (tashbih).
- **Strength:** Strong; this is the fundamental "Scale" (*mizan*) Ibn al-Arabi uses to avoid both pure abstraction and anthropomorphism.

### 2. Ontology: The World of Imagination (*Alam al-Khayal*)
- **What is argued:** Imagination is not "unreal" but an intermediate ontological realm (*barzakh*) where meanings take on forms and bodies are spiritualized. It is the "location" of the soul and the realm of visionary experience.
- **Support:** Philosophical analysis of "Being" (*Wujud*) and "Nonexistence" (*Adam*), supported by prophetic traditions (hadith) about dreams and the afterlife.
- **Strength:** Strong; the core of Chittick's contribution to modern Ibn al-Arabi studies.

### 3. Epistemology: Unveiling (*Kashf*) and Reason (*'Aql*)
- **What is argued:** While reason (*'Aql*) is necessary for "binding" and defining things (tanzih), it is limited and cannot grasp the Divine in its similarity (tashbih). True knowledge comes through "Unveiling" (*Kashf*) or "Opening" (*Futuh*), which transcends the limits of rational thought.
- **Support:** Extensive citations from the *Futuhat* regarding the "Scale of the Law" and the "Scale of Reason."
- **Strength:** Strong; central to Ibn al-Arabi's self-understanding as a "follower" of the Prophet rather than a philosopher.

### 4. Soteriology: The Perfect Human (*Al-Insan al-Kamil*)
- **What is argued:** The human being is a microcosm of the entire universe and a mirror for all Divine Names. The "Perfect Human" is the one who has fully realized this potential and serves as the channel for Divine grace into the world.
- **Support:** The metaphysical interpretation of the creation of Adam "in God's image" and the cosmic role of the Prophet Muhammad.
- **Strength:** Strong; provides the spiritual goal for the entire Akbari system.

## What this source adds
- **Direct Translation:** Unlike earlier works that relied on secondary summaries, Chittick lets Ibn al-Arabi speak for himself through hundreds of pages of direct translation.
- **Technical Rigor:** It defines and distinguishes complex Arabic terms (e.g., *wujud*, *thubut*, *ayan al-thabita*) with unprecedented precision.
- **Systematic Structure:** It organizes the "ocean" of the *Futuhat* into a logical progression that is accessible to scholars and serious practitioners.

## Limitations and Biases
- **Scholarly Focus:** The book is highly dense and technical, which may be daunting for general readers.
- **Pro-Akbari Perspective:** Chittick is a sympathetic expositor; while he acknowledges critics, his primary goal is to present Ibn al-Arabi's system on its own terms.
- **Limited Historical Context:** The book focuses almost exclusively on the text and metaphysics, providing less detail on the social or political history of the 13th century.
